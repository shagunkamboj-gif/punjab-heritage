The Art of Punjabi Embroidery

Punjab, the "Land of Five Rivers," has nurtured a breathtaking tradition of hand embroidery for over five centuries. Every motif, every thread, and every color tells a story ‚Äî of harvest celebrations, monsoon joys, and the unbreakable spirit of its people.

From the delicate florals of Phulkari to the geometric brilliance of Bagh, Punjabi embroidery is more than craft ‚Äî it is a living, breathing chronicle of culture passed down through generations of women artisans.

The Crown Jewel

Phulkari ‚Äî The Flower Work

Phulkari, literally meaning "flower work," is Punjab's most iconic embroidery tradition ‚Äî a dazzling art form where silk threads bloom into geometric and floral masterpieces on hand-spun cloth.

Bagh ‚Äî The Garden

When Phulkari embroidery is so dense that it entirely covers the base fabric, it becomes a Bagh ‚Äî a "garden" of thread. These spectacular pieces take months to complete and are considered family heirlooms, often passed from mother to daughter as symbols of blessings and prosperity.

Chope ‚Äî The Bridal Blessing

The Chope is a sacred Phulkari created specifically for brides. Gifted by the grandmother, it is draped over the bride during the wedding ceremony as a symbol of love and protection. Embroidered only on one side, its patterns carry prayers for a blissful married life.

History & Origin

The origins of Phulkari trace back to the 15th century in the Punjab region, though some scholars believe its roots are even more ancient. Early references appear in the Punjabi epic Heer Ranjha by Waris Shah (1766), where the heroine Heer is described wearing exquisite Phulkari.

Traditionally, Phulkari was not a commercial craft ‚Äî it was a deeply personal art practiced by women of every household. A mother would begin embroidering a Phulkari for her daughter the moment the girl was born, completing it in time for her wedding. The darning stitch (holbein) worked from the reverse side of the fabric is the hallmark technique that gives Phulkari its distinctive raised, textured appearance.

Today, efforts by artisan cooperatives and cultural organizations continue to preserve this UNESCO-recognized art, ensuring it thrives for generations to come.

Hand Embroidery

Phulkari is worked entirely by hand using the darning stitch. Artisans embroider from the reverse side of the fabric, creating raised patterns on the front. A single piece can take 3 to 6 months to complete, with each stitch placed with extraordinary precision.

Materials

The base fabric is typically hand-spun khaddar (handloom cotton) or coarse cotton in rich, earthy tones. The embroidery thread is pat ‚Äî an untwisted silk floss in vibrant hues of gold, magenta, green, orange, and royal blue.

Role of Artisans

Historically, every Punjabi woman was a Phulkari artisan. Today, dedicated women artisans in rural Punjab keep this heritage alive. Organizations and cooperatives empower them through fair-trade practices, ensuring their art reaches global audiences while sustaining livelihoods.
